Lacrosse and the 2028 Olympics:
What It Means for Europe

The return of lacrosse to the Olympics is reshaping priorities, investment and visibility across the European scene. An analysis of who stands to gain most.

✍

Thomas Renard

Analysis Editor, FTL

📅 Feb 10, 2026
🕐 10 min read
📍 Europe
Share f 𝕏 in 🔗

When World Lacrosse confirmed that Sixes lacrosse would be part of the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics programme, the reaction across Europe was not uniformly excited. It was, for many national federations, alarming. They had programmes built around Field lacrosse. Now the Olympic format was something different.

The Sixes problem

Sixes lacrosse shares its fundamental equipment and most of its rules with Field lacrosse. But it plays very differently: six players per side, a 30-second shot clock, a smaller field. The skill sets overlap, but the tactical instincts required are distinct.

For European nations that had spent decades building Field programmes, the Olympic announcement created an immediate fork in the road: continue developing Field, which has the established club infrastructure and fan base, or redirect resources toward Sixes, which has the Olympic spotlight.

The answer, for most federations, has been a combination of both — with varying degrees of coherence.

Who is best positioned

Netherlands and Spain have the clearest head start. Both scenes had already committed to Sixes before the announcement, and both have the club infrastructure to sustain meaningful domestic competition in the format. England, with its Box programme, also has attributes that transfer well to Sixes.

Germany, Italy and France face a more complex transition. All have substantial Field programmes that generate the bulk of their playing membership. Pivoting toward Sixes without disrupting what already works is a genuine governance challenge.

🏅 Lacrosse and the 2028 Olympics

Sixes lacrosse at Los Angeles 2028

Lacrosse returns to the Olympic Games at Los Angeles 2028 — absent since 1908 — in the Sixes format. For European programmes, qualification means navigating both continental rankings and a global process that heavily favours North American nations. The format itself — six players per side, a condensed field — is also new to many European players.

The Olympics is a once-in-a-generation opportunity. But chasing it without a plan is how you end up with two weak programmes instead of one strong one.

— Anonymous federation president, speaking to FTL

File image. © Unsplash

💡 Why this matters

The Olympic cycle will redraw the European lacrosse map

The nations that navigate the Sixes transition most effectively in the next two years will likely lead European lacrosse for a decade. This is not just about 2028 — it is about which programmes emerge from that cycle with increased funding, visibility and youth participation.
